Bristol zoo monkeys caught secretly sharing ONE Amazon account – and keepers can't quite believe it

Keepers were baffled by a flood of parcels addressed to 'The Lads, Enclosure 4' – before uncovering the troop's elaborate subscription scheme

By Zoo & Wildlife Correspondent · 06:00, 16 JUN 2026

A troop of monkeys at a Bristol zoo have been rumbled secretly sharing a single Amazon Prime account – leaving keepers stunned at just how organised the cheeky primates had become.

Staff at the attraction were left scratching their heads this week after a suspicious number of parcels began turning up at the primate house, all addressed to "The Lads, Enclosure 4".

Inside the boxes, keepers say they found a banana slicer, a weighted blanket and three copies of a self-help book titled Stop Monkeying Around.

Head keeper Denise Florr said the troop had managed to keep the whole thing quiet for weeks.

"We always knew they were close. But we had no idea they'd gone in together on a subscription. It's the most organised thing I've seen all year."

'More organised than our staff room'

According to staff, the ringleader is an enterprising capuchin named Gerald, who is said to have set up the account, added the rest of the troop as authorised users, and demanded they pay him back each month for their share.

The spider monkeys, keepers admit, kept dodging their half of the bill.

CCTV from the enclosure reportedly showed the troop gathered around a tablet most evenings, bingeing nature documentaries late into the night – though they are said to have consistently skipped anything featuring snakes.

"The free delivery was the real draw," Ms Florr explained. "When you've got no thumbs and no wallet, next-day shipping is a genuine game-changer.

"They'd worked out the whole system. Honestly, it was more organised than our staff break room."

Tantrum over device limit

Trouble only started, staff say, when the account hit its device limit and a junior macaque was locked out halfway through a documentary.

One witness described the resulting scene as "an absolutely textbook tantrum, banana and all".

But zoo management say they are taking a relaxed view of the whole affair.

"We're not going to ape outrage over it," a spokesperson said. "They've shown teamwork, budgeting and a frankly impressive grasp of subscription economics.

"We're now seriously considering putting them in charge of the gift shop."

The account has since been switched to a family plan. Gerald, BristolLive understands, remains the responsible adult.

As for what staff are now calling the troop? Ms Florr didn't hesitate. "Well," she grinned, "they really are prime mates."
